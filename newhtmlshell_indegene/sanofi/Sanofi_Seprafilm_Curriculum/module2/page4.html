Tissue Manipulation
Tissue manipulation occurs during surgery where surgeons move organs and tissues out of the way. Even the slightest tissue abrasion can damage the monocellular mesothelium and cause the peritoneal healing process to begin, thus leading to adhesion formation.
Desiccation
Mesothelial tissue is normally kept moist when the body is closed, but it is exposed to the drying effect of air during surgery. This condition is known as desiccation, which is one of the causes for postsurgical adhesions.
Contact with Blood
The tissue may also come into contact with blood during surgery. This increases the available amount of fibrin at the surface of the damaged tissue, and white blood cells exacerbate the inflammatory response precipitating adhesion formation.
Abrasion
Abrasion or sharp trauma to the peritoneum disrupts the confluent layer of mesothelial cells, leading to postsurgical adhesion formation.
Heat or Electrocautery
Many surgical procedures involve the use of electrically generated heat to cauterize bleeding vessels or to cut tissues. Thermal injuries can lead to both ischemia and inflammation, which in turn results adhesion formation.
Foreign Particles
Although surgical supplies are sterile, surface particles can adhere to the operative site. For example, starch used to lubricate surgical gloves, lint from swabs, and paper fibers from gowns can trigger an inflammatory response, leading to adhesion formation.
Suturing
Whenever sutures or staples are placed in the peritoneum, blood flow to the peritoneum can be compromised if the sutures are too tight, or the sutures themselves may provoke a foreign body reaction, precipitating the formation of adhesions.
Infection
Infection and inflammatory disease may result in adhesion formation.
